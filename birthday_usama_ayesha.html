§ 03 — The Sister's Manifesto

A letter
you didn't ask for.

Bhai — of all the people I could have been born after, I am extraordinarily lucky it was you. You are the first person who ever made me feel like my thoughts mattered, even when I was small enough to be ignored.

"You taught me that silence can be the loudest form of strength — and that protecting someone doesn't always look like a shield. Sometimes it looks like just being there."

You never had to be loud about it. You never made a show of being the older one. You just quietly showed up — in the right rooms, at the right times, with the right calm. I have been watching you do that my whole life, and I think I forgot to tell you: I see it. I always saw it.

"The funniest part of our story is that we share a birthday — and yet you managed to make every single June 27 feel like it was entirely mine. That is the most generous thing a person can do."

On this birthday — our birthday — I want you to know that every lesson you ever gave me (half of them probably not intentional) is something I carry. I carry your patience, your quiet confidence, your terrible early taste in music before Talha Anjum fixed it, and your absolute inability to lose an argument gracefully.

You are 365 days of year I'm grateful to have an older brother, and today — June 27 — I get to say it out loud: Happy Birthday, Usama Malik. And happy birthday to us.

— Ayesha ♡

§ 08 — Sibling Lore

Things that
cannot be explained to outsiders.

A curated archive of the most consequential moments in shared history.

ARGUMENT #001
The Bigger Cake Slice Incident
+

Every year. Every single June 27. The moment the birthday cake arrived on the table, the negotiations began. Usama always said he should get more because he was older. Ayesha always said she should get more because she was younger and therefore in need of more calories. This argument has never been resolved and never will be. The real winner is whoever cuts the cake — which is now a privilege neither of them will delegate to the other.

PHRASE #002
"Pehle tumhara bhi tha."
+

The phrase that has been deployed approximately 847 times to claim ownership of: the TV remote, the better seat in the car, the last samosa, the comfortable chair, the blanket, and once — memorably — a parking space that neither of them was even driving to. "It was mine first" is a worldview, not just an argument. This phrase is Usama's founding philosophy. Ayesha has learned to use it back.

RITUAL #003
The Shared Midnight Countdown
+

Neither of them talks about this much. But every June 26 at 11:59pm, there's a shared awareness — a moment before the clock turns — that belongs to only them. No announcements, no posts, no performances. Just the quiet understanding that in exactly one minute, both of them will be a year older at the exact same second. That moment is the real birthday. Everything else is cake.

MEMORY #004
When He Let Her Win
+

There have been exactly three arguments Usama clearly won but conceded gracefully. Ayesha noticed every single time. She never said anything. He probably doesn't know she noticed. This memory is being documented here as official record: she knows. She has always known. And it is one of the things she will carry quietly for the rest of her life — the small evidence of how much he cared, dressed up as losing.

TRADITION #005
The "Whose Birthday Is It Really" Annual Debate
+

Held annually. Judged by no one. Winner declared by both simultaneously. The official position, now in its final form after years of litigation: it is both of their birthdays, the universe didn't ask for opinions when it scheduled this, and whoever makes breakfast first gets to say it's primarily theirs. This year's winner: whoever reads this first. (It's a tie. It's always a tie.)

§ 09 — The Protector Archive

He never called
it that. But it was.

U
The Anchor
What protection looks like

Not always armour. Sometimes it's a phone call at 11pm when he somehow knows something's off. A quiet "how are you actually" that has a different weight than everyone else's version.

The presence without announcement

He doesn't announce that he's there. He's just always already there — in the advice he gives without making you feel like you needed it, in the way he handles things quietly so others don't have to.

What Ayesha carries from this

A belief that safety doesn't have to be loud. That someone can hold space for you without holding you back. That the best version of being looked after feels exactly like freedom.

"Having an older brother like you is like having a pilot light — always on, almost invisible, but the reason the whole house stays warm."

— Ayesha, June 27
